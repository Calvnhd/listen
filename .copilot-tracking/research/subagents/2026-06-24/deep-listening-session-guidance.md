# Subagent Research: Deep / Critical Listening Session Guidance

## Research Topics & Questions

Practical, actionable guidance for conducting deep/critical/attentive listening sessions to
recorded MUSIC and AMBIENT/ENVIRONMENTAL SOUND, to inform a software tool that guides users
through deep listening sessions (prompts, methods, note-taking). NOT interpersonal listening.

1. How-to guidance for a deep/critical listening session
   - Preparation: environment, equipment (headphones vs speakers), volume, time of day, session length
   - Listening posture/mindset: single-tasking, eyes closed, removing distractions, intention-setting
   - Structured approaches: multiple passes with focal points (melody, bass, rhythm, timbre, lyrics,
     arrangement, dynamics, stereo image, production); zoom in / zoom out
   - Critical listening / ear training traditions (Jason Corey; Harman / train-the-ears); dimensions
     trained listeners attend to (frequency balance, dynamics, reverb/space, distortion, stereo width)
   - Active listening for music appreciation (Copland's three planes: sensuous, expressive, sheerly musical)
2. Note-taking & reflection during/after listening (journals, structured vs freeform, rating/tagging, prompts)
3. Common mistakes & pitfalls (passive creep, multitasking, over-intellectualizing, ear fatigue,
   biases — expectation/confirmation/loudness/familiarity, poor/inconsistent environment, doing too much)
4. Getting the most from single & multiple sessions over time (progression, habit, revisiting,
   comparative listening, vocabulary, tracking growth)

## Findings

### Copland — Three Planes of Listening (music appreciation foundation)
Source: Aaron Copland, *What to Listen for in Music* / essay "How We Listen To Music".
Three planes (he stresses we listen on all three simultaneously, never just one):
- **Sensuous plane** — listening for the sheer pleasure of the sound itself; the most basic,
  pleasurable level; can become passive/background "bath of sound" if you stop there.
- **Expressive plane** — the meaning/emotion behind the notes; what the music expresses or evokes
  (mood, narrative, feeling). Copland: music does have meaning but it can't always be pinned to words.
- **Sheerly musical plane** — the notes themselves and their manipulation: melody, harmony, rhythm,
  tone color (timbre), form/structure. Conscious, analytical attention to musical materials.
Actionable for tool: offer passes mapped to each plane (enjoy → feel → analyze), and remind users
the goal is to listen "more consciously/attentively" on all three at once, not to dissect mechanically.
Sources for this: springmuse.hunter.cuny.edu PDF (primary text, blocked from extraction but title/quote
confirmed); Quizlet study guide; Spokane Falls CC LibGuide; mindjournal.blog summary; thesublimeblog.org.

### Pauline Oliveros — Deep Listening (10-step guide, Crack Magazine, Dan Johnson, 20.06.25)
URL: https://crackmagazine.net/article/lists/10-step-guide-deep-listening/
Core distinction: **Hearing is passive; listening is active** ("giving our attention"). Oliveros
coined "deep listening" in 1988 after recording in a cave. Defined (RPI Center) as "listening in every
possible way to everything possible to hear ... exploring the relationships among any and all sounds."
The 10 principles (highly actionable, map directly to prompts):
1. Remember listening is active — we can't listen mindlessly; it is giving attention.
2. **Hone in on one thing at a time** — single-task. "If it's listening to music, then just listen to
   the music. Set aside time and just listen to a whole album. You don't have to be scrolling."
3. Listen for music in the world (ambient/environmental sound; birds, valleys, construction).
4. **Pay attention to how each sound makes you feel** — withhold judgment; notice where sound resonates
   in your body, what feelings/thoughts arise.
5. Consider the bigger picture (personal, social, ecological context of what you consume).
6. Move your body to music (embodied listening, community).
7. **Train your listening** — "how much is actually happening within that sound?" Listen to a whole
   soundscape, then zoom: start with sounds closest to you, gradually expand outward (ZOOM IN/OUT).
8. **Go to extremes** — start with minimalism/noise or sparse material ("something with less going on")
   before dense pop; easier to hear detail when there's less happening.
9. Embrace dreams (set intention before sleep; record what you remember).
10. Tune into silence (sit with silence; uncomfortable but valuable).
RPI "Center for Deep Listening" (deeplistening.rpi.edu) offers formal training programs.

### Yamaha / Mike Levine (2020) + George Massenburg — "How to Develop Critical Listening Skills"
URL: https://hub.yamaha.com/audio/a-how-to/how-to-develop-critical-listening-skills/
Massenburg: "Hearing is the act of perceiving sound by the ear. Listening is a conscious mental
process, as much about the brain as the ear" (paper "Critical Listening and Critical Evaluation," 2019).
Three "listen like a…" lenses (great as structured passes):
- **Listen like a PRODUCER** — evaluate songwriting (music+lyrics), arrangement, performance quality;
  become an aficionado of the genre (read interviews, watch/listen to background) to judge in context.
- **Listen like an ARRANGER** — pick out individual instruments; "play a song repeatedly as you
  concentrate on one instrument at a time." Hear how parts interlock; notice genre conventions.
- **Listen like an ENGINEER** — technical analysis: panning/stereo placement & virtual soundstage
  (what's center vs L/R, forward vs back via level and reverb amount), frequency/tonal balance
  (clarity vs muddy; sizzly/boxy/boomy = too much high/mid/low), effects (reverb = diffuse cloud vs
  delay = distinct echoes; natural vs processed voice), distortion/processing artifacts/over-compression,
  and **dynamics** (dynamic vs static = everything same volume from over-compression/limiting).
- **"Change is good"** — listen for how the arrangement builds and changes over time (instruments added,
  intensity varies, peak near the end); dynamics shifting holds interest.
Use ears not eyes (don't rely on a frequency analyzer).

### Critical-listening / ear-training texts & courses (dimensions trained listeners attend to)
- **Jason Corey, *Audio Production and Critical Listening: Technical Ear Training*** (Routledge; 2nd/3rd
  eds w/ David Benson). Focus: identify by ear — parametric EQ & tonal balance, dynamics processing,
  reverberation/spatial audio, distortion & noise. Comes with "Technical Ear Trainer" software practice
  modules (EQ, dynamics, reverb, distortion, delay). Site: sites.google.com/a/umich.edu/jason-corey.
  taylorfrancis.com/books/mono/10.4324/9781003461173 ; revised ed adds parametric EQ/tonal balance,
  spatial audio & reverberation, dynamics, distortion & noise.
- **Berklee Online "Critical Listening 1"** — recognize EQ, compression, reverb, delay, modulation,
  distortion, panning and connect to stylistic intent; "structured critical listening frameworks to
  diagnose mix issues." Goal described as heightened music listening and awareness + vocabulary.
- Common trained dimensions across sources: **frequency/tonal balance, dynamics (compression/transients),
  reverb/space/depth, distortion/artifacts, stereo width/imaging/panning**.
