## Findings

### 1. Pauline Oliveros & "Deep Listening"

- Pauline Oliveros (1932–2016), American composer/accordionist, central figure in post-war experimental & electronic music; founding member & director of San Francisco Tape Music Center. Coined "deep listening" in 1988 after descending ~14 ft into the Dan Harpole underground cistern (Port Townsend, WA) to make a recording — originally a pun on the depth of the cistern. (Wikipedia: Pauline Oliveros)
- "Deep listening" blossomed into "an aesthetic...designed to inspire both trained and untrained performers to practice the art of listening and responding to environmental conditions in solo and ensemble situations." (Wikipedia quoting Jason Ankeny bio)
- Deep Listening Band formed by Oliveros, Stuart Dempster, Panaiotis (later David Gamper); specialized in performing/recording in resonant/reverberant spaces — caves, cathedrals, underground cisterns. (Wikipedia)
- Deep Listening became a program of the Pauline Oliveros Foundation (founded 1985); included annual listening **retreats** (Europe, New Mexico, upstate NY), plus **apprenticeship and certification** programs. Foundation renamed **Deep Listening Institute, Ltd.** in 2005. Now stewarded by the **Center for Deep Listening at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute (RPI)**, Troy, NY. (Wikipedia)
- Key book: Pauline Oliveros, *Deep Listening: A Composer's Sound Practice* (iUniverse, 2005). Other writings: *Sonic Meditations* (1971), *Software for People* (1984), *Sounding the Margins* (2010), *Anthology of Text Scores*.
- **"Sonic awareness"** (theory named by Heidi Von Gunden): "the ability to consciously focus attention upon environmental and musical sound," requiring "continual alertness and an inclination to be always listening." Compared to John Berger's visual consciousness ("Ways of Seeing"). Described as "a synthesis of the psychology of consciousness, the physiology of the martial arts, and the sociology of the feminist movement." (Von Gunden 1980/1983, via Wikipedia)
- Oliveros distinguishes two modes of processing: **"attention and awareness"** = **focal attention** vs **global attention**, symbolized by a **dot (focal/exclusive) and a circle (global/inclusive)**. Later quartered into: actively making sound, actively imagining sound, listening to present sound, remembering past sound (model used in Sonic Meditations). (Von Gunden via Wikipedia)
- Hearing vs. listening (Oliveros's well-known formulation, paraphrased; confirmed by the Center for Deep Listening / *Quantum Listening*, 1999): hearing is the involuntary physical means of perception; listening is the conscious act of giving attention to what is perceived, "both acoustically and psychologically." Deep Listening = "listening in as many ways as possible to everything that can possibly be heard all of the time" and "going below the surface of what is heard, expanding to the whole field of sound while finding focus." (Center for Deep Listening, quoting Oliveros's "Quantum Listening," 1999)
- Oliveros developed the attentional process with physicist/karate master Lester Ingber at UCSD; studied karate (black belt). Body/martial-arts grounding is integral. (Wikipedia)
- Oliveros's definition of "soundscape": "All of the waveforms faithfully transmitted to our audio cortex by the ear and its mechanisms." (Oliveros 2005, *Deep Listening*, p.18, cited in Wikipedia: Soundscape)

### 2a. Pierre Schaeffer — reduced listening, sound object, four modes

- Pierre Schaeffer (1910–1995), French composer/engineer/musicologist; founder of **musique concrète** (term coined 1948) and GRM (Groupe de Recherches Musicales). Key text: *Traité des objets musicaux* (1966); also *À la recherche d'une musique concrète* (1952) / *In Search of a Concrete Music*. (Wikipedia: Pierre Schaeffer)
- **Sound object (l'objet sonore)**: "a primary unit of sonic material," often specifically recorded sound rather than notated music; coined in *Traité des objets musicaux* (1966). "This unit of sound is the equivalent to a unit of breath or articulation, a unit of instrumental gesture. The sound object is therefore an acoustic action and intention of listening." Should be freed from its sonic origin/source so a listener cannot identify it = acousmatic listening. (Wikipedia: Sound object)
- **Reduced listening (écoute réduite)**: listening that focuses on the traits of the sound itself, independent of its cause and meaning. Uses a **phenomenological** approach (epoché/reduction) derived from Husserl & Merleau-Ponty. The sound object "is never revealed clearly except in the acousmatic experience" (Schaeffer, via Brian Kane, *Sound Unseen*). (Wikipedia: Sound object; Acousmatic sound)
- **Schaeffer's four modes / "four functions of listening"** (the French verbs): **ouïr** (to hear — passive perception/detection by the autonomous mechanism of hearing), **écouter** (to listen — actively perceive, treat sound as index/clue to its source/event), **entendre** (to hear-with-intention/perceive selectively — qualitative listening to chosen aspects of the sound object), **comprendre** (to understand — grasp meaning, treat sound as sign/language, abstraction & comparison). (Wikipedia: Sound object lists the four-step "What Can be Heard": detection by ouïr; deciphering by écouter; twofold focused attention judging/describing; significance understood by abstraction.)
- **Acousmatic**: Schaeffer (with Jérôme Peignot, 1955) revived the Greek *akousmatikoi* (Pythagoras's probationary students who listened behind a veil). Defined in *Traité* (1966:91): "referring to a sound that one hears without seeing the causes behind it." Schaeffer: "Often surprised, often uncertain, we discover that much of what we thought we were hearing, was in reality only seen, and explained, by the context" (1966:93). Acousmatic reduction "redirects awareness to hearing alone." (Wikipedia: Acousmatic sound)
- Related extensions: Denis Smalley's **spectromorphology** (analyzing the spectral & morphological shape of sounds as heard) and **source bonding** (the tendency to relate sounds to their sources — extrinsic when source-bond intact, intrinsic when not). (Wikipedia: Sound object)

### 2b. Michel Chion — three listening modes (film/recorded sound)

- Michel Chion (b. 1947), French film theorist & musique concrète composer; assistant to Schaeffer at GRM (1971–76). Key text: *L'audio-vision* (1990) / *Audio-Vision: Sound on Screen* (trans. Claudia Gorbman, Columbia UP, 1994). (Wikipedia: Michel Chion)
- Chion's **three listening modes** (derived from / extending Schaeffer): **causal listening** (listening to a sound to gather info about its cause/source — most common, least reliable), **semantic listening** (listening to interpret a code/language, e.g. speech, Morse), **reduced listening** (Schaeffer's term — listening to the traits of the sound itself, independent of cause and meaning). *(Detail to confirm from primary excerpt — see next batch.)*
- **Acousmatic in film**: source seen first then "acousmatized," OR sound initially acousmatic then source revealed ("de-acousmatization"). The visualised vs. acousmatic opposition underpins on-screen/off-screen space. (Wikipedia: Acousmatic sound, citing Chion 1994:72–73)

### 2c. R. Murray Schafer & acoustic ecology / soundscape

- R. Murray Schafer (1933–2021), Canadian composer/educator/environmentalist; founded soundscape studies at Simon Fraser University in the 1960s; led the **World Soundscape Project** (with Barry Truax, Hildegard Westerkamp, Bruce Davis, Peter Huse). Key book: *The Tuning of the World* (1977), republished as *The Soundscape: Our Sonic Environment and the Tuning of the World* (1994). (Wikipedia: R. Murray Schafer; Soundscape; Acoustic ecology)
- **Soundscape** = the acoustic environment as perceived by humans, in context (ISO 12913-1:2014 standardized the definition). Term popularized (not coined) by Schafer; he attributes it to city planner Michael Southworth ("The Sonic Environment of Cities," 1967).
- **Three elements of a soundscape** (Schafer): **Keynote sounds** (background sounds, often not consciously perceived, "outline the character" of a place — e.g., nature sounds; in cities, traffic); **Sound signals** (foreground sounds listened to consciously — bells, whistles, horns, sirens, warning devices); **Soundmark** (from "landmark": a sound unique to an area, deserving protection: "once a Soundmark has been identified, it deserves to be protected, for soundmarks make the acoustic life of a community unique"). (Wikipedia: Soundscape)
- **Hi-fi vs lo-fi soundscapes**: hi-fi = favorable signal-to-noise ratio, discrete sounds heard clearly, perspective/distance audible (rural); lo-fi = signals obscured by dense noise, no perspective, everything close/compact (urban). (Wikipedia: Soundscape)
- **Schizophonia** (Schafer, 1969): the splitting of a sound from its source — "We have split the sound from the maker of the sound. Sounds have been torn from their natural sockets..." Describes recorded/reproduced/amplified sound. (Wikipedia: R. Murray Schafer)
- **Clairaudience** (Schafer): "clear hearing" — a (re)cultivated, exceptional clarity of hearing, especially of environmental sound; an aim of ear cleaning. *(Term to confirm exact Schafer definition — appears in Tuning of the World.)*
- **Ear cleaning**: Schafer's term & 1967 book *Ear Cleaning: Notes for an Experimental Music Course* — exercises to (cognitively) clean/sensitize the ears, re-engage aural awareness. Schafer also wrote *A Sound Education: 100 Exercises in Listening and Soundmaking* (1992) and *The Thinking Ear*. (Wikipedia: R. Murray Schafer; Soundwalk)
- **Soundwalk** = "any excursion whose main purpose is listening to the environment... exposing our ears to every sound around us no matter where we are" (Hildegard Westerkamp, "Soundwalking," 1974). Term first used by WSP members under Schafer (Vancouver, 1970s). Schafer saw soundwalking as part of ear-cleaning/re-engaging aural senses. (Wikipedia: Soundwalk)
- **Bernie Krause** redefined soundscape sources as **geophony** (non-biological natural: wind, water, earth movement), **biophony** (non-human biological: birdsong, animal calls), **anthropophony/anthrophony** (human-generated sound). Acoustic niche hypothesis (1993): species partition acoustic space by frequency/time. (Wikipedia: Soundscape; Acoustic ecology)
- **World Listening Day**: held annually July 18 (Schafer's birthday) since 2010, organized by the World Listening Project. (Wikipedia: R. Murray Schafer)
- **World Soundscape Project** founded by Schafer at SFU late 1960s; grew out of a course on noise pollution; produced *The Vancouver Soundscape* (1973), *Five Village Soundscapes* (1977), *European Sound Diary*; team incl. Barry Truax, Hildegard Westerkamp, Bruce Davis, Peter Huse, Howard Broomfield. Truax's *Handbook for Acoustic Ecology* (1978) standardized terminology. WFAE founded 1993. (sfu.ca/~truax World Soundscape Project)

### 2d. Eric Clarke — ecological approach to listening

- Eric F. Clarke (b. 1955), Heather Professor of Music, University of Oxford (since 2007); PhD in Psychology (Exeter); music psychologist. Book: *Ways of Listening: An Ecological Approach to the Perception of Musical Meaning* (Oxford University Press, 2005). (Wikipedia: Eric Clarke)
- Applies J.J. Gibson's **ecological theory of perception** to music: perception is **direct** — listeners "pick up" information from a richly structured acoustic array rather than constructing/inferring meaning from impoverished data. Music presents **affordances** (what the sonic environment offers/invites the listener to do/feel) and **perceptual invariants** that specify sources and meanings. Emphasis on the **active, embodied, mobile listener** and the **mutuality** of perceiver and environment. Reframes musical meaning as arising in the encounter between listener and sound, bridging "autonomist"/formalist and contextual approaches. (Clarke 2005; subtitle conveys the thesis.)

### 2e. Adorno — structural listening (and listener typology)

- Theodor W. Adorno (1903–1969), Frankfurt School philosopher and music critic; classically trained (composition with Alban Berg). Key listening texts: **"On the Fetish-Character in Music and the Regression of Listening"** (1938) and **Introduction to the Sociology of Music** (1962). (Wikipedia: Theodor W. Adorno)
- **Structural listening** (the term associated with Adorno's ideal of "adequate"/full listening): the proper way to listen to serious music is to grasp the **whole work as a coherent musical structure**, following its logic and the relation of parts to the whole in real time — not fixating on isolated melodies, effects, or recognizable "hooks." Adorno: serious music "achieves excellence when its whole is greater than the sum of its parts"; Beethoven's greatness lies in "the complete subordination of the accidentally private melodic elements to the form as a whole." (Wikipedia: Theodor W. Adorno, "Standardization" section)
- Contrasted with **"regression of listening"** / distracted, fetishistic, atomistic listening fostered by the **culture industry** (recognition of standardized hooks, passive consumption). In *Introduction to the Sociology of Music* Adorno gives a **typology of listeners** ("types of musical conduct"): expert (fully structural) → good listener → culture consumer → emotional listener → resentment listener → jazz listener → entertainment listener → indifferent/unmusical. (Wikipedia: Theodor W. Adorno)
- The concept was later critiqued: Rose Rosengard Subotnik, "Toward a Deconstruction of Structural Listening" (in *Deconstructive Variations*, 1996); Andrew Dell'Antonio (ed.), *Beyond Structural Listening? Postmodern Modes of Hearing* (2004). (scholarly context)

### 2f. Aaron Copland — three planes of listening

- Aaron Copland, *What to Listen for in Music* (1939): we listen on **three planes simultaneously** — (1) **sensuous plane** (the sheer pleasure of the sound itself, with no thought of meaning); (2) **expressive plane** (the feeling/mood/meaning behind the notes); (3) **sheerly musical plane** (the notes themselves and their manipulation — melody, harmony, rhythm, tone color, form). The ideal/active listener moves among all three and especially cultivates the often-neglected sheerly musical plane (consciously following the musical material). (Copland 1939 — classic music-appreciation framework; widely reproduced.)

### 3. Concrete METHODS & TECHNIQUES for deep/attentive listening

- **Soundwalk** (Westerkamp/WSP): an excursion whose sole purpose is listening to the environment; can be solo or group, often in silence, sometimes with a focus prompt (e.g., listen for the quietest sound, follow one sound, count sound sources). Used as acoustic-ecology fieldwork and as a sensitizing practice. (Wikipedia: Soundwalk)
- **Ear cleaning** (Schafer): graduated listening exercises to "clean"/sensitize the ears. *Ear Cleaning* (1967) and *A Sound Education: 100 Exercises in Listening and Soundmaking* (1992). Typical exercises: list every sound you can hear and mark which you'd never noticed; find the highest and lowest sounds in a space; identify keynote vs signal vs soundmark; keep a "sound diary"; imitate sounds; locate sounds with eyes closed. (Wikipedia: R. Murray Schafer; Soundwalk)
- **Sonic Meditations** (Oliveros, 1971): text/verbal scores prescribing listening and/or sounding actions, typically done as a group (originally the ♀ Ensemble), often with **eyes closed**, attention to **breath and body**. Famous example **"Teach Yourself to Fly"**: sit in a circle, observe your own breathing; gradually allow the breath to become audible and then vocalized; let the voice find its own pitches; listen to the whole evolving sound field. Four-fold model used: actively making sound / actively imagining sound / listening to present sound / remembering past sound. (Center for Deep Listening; Wikipedia: Pauline Oliveros)
- **Deep Listening practice** (Oliveros): combines **bodywork**, sonic meditations, **dream work**, and listening to the sounds of daily life, nature, one's own thoughts and imagination; processed through **group discussion** in workshops and retreats; "going below the surface of what is heard, expanding to the whole field of sound while finding focus." Previous musical training not required. (Center for Deep Listening; *Deep Listening: A Composer's Sound Practice*, 2005)
- **Inclusive (global/open-awareness) vs exclusive (focal) attention** (Oliveros): the core attentional technique — alternately **opening** to the whole sound field (the "circle") and **narrowing** to a single sound or feature (the "dot"), deliberately switching between and sustaining each. "We open in order to listen to the world as a field of possibilities and we listen with narrowed attention for specific things of vital interest to us." (Oliveros, "Quantum Listening," 1999, via Center for Deep Listening)
- **Reduced listening** (Schaeffer/Chion): **repeated listening** to a fixed/recorded sound to describe its **intrinsic** qualities (pitch, timbre, grain, attack/dynamics, morphology), bracketing source and meaning. Enabled by **acousmatic playback** (loudspeakers, recorded sound — the "veil") and by **repetition** of the recorded fragment. (Wikipedia: Sound object; Acousmatic sound; Chion, *Audio-Vision*, 1994)
- **Repeat-listening passes with shifting focal points** (applied attentive-music-listening, consistent with Copland's planes + Schaeffer's parameters + Oliveros's focal/global switching): listen to the same piece several times, each pass attending to a different layer/parameter — e.g., one instrument/voice → rhythm/groove → melody → harmony → timbre/texture → spatial qualities (stereo image, depth, reverb/space) → overall form/structure over time. (Synthesis of Copland 1939; Schaeffer; Oliveros.)
- **Eyes-closed listening, posture, breath, silence**: eyes closed reduces visual dominance and supports acousmatic/reduced attention; comfortable-but-alert posture; breath as an attentional anchor (per Sonic Meditations); framing silences before and after to reset and bound the listening. (Oliveros practice; acousmatic tradition.)

### 4. GOALS & PURPOSE of deep listening

- **Heightened awareness / presence**: expand perception toward "everything that can possibly be heard all of the time"; cultivate "continual alertness and an inclination to be always listening" (sonic awareness). (Oliveros; Von Gunden)
- **Distinguish hearing from listening**: move from the involuntary/physical (hearing) to the conscious/intentional/psychological (listening). (Oliveros; Center for Deep Listening)
- **Discover detail and structure** otherwise missed; perceive the sound object or the musical work on its own terms (reduced/structural listening). (Schaeffer; Adorno; Chion)
- **Aesthetic & emotional depth, connection**: connect with the acoustic environment, all that inhabits it, others, and oneself; "deep listening takes us below the surface of our consciousness and helps to change or dissolve limiting boundaries." (Oliveros)
- **Contemplative/meditative practice**: meditation, improvisation, creativity, collaboration, playfulness, community growth. (Center for Deep Listening)
- **Acoustic-ecology aims**: clairaudience (clear hearing), re-engaging the aural sense, awareness of and care for the soundscape (protecting soundmarks), and critique of noise pollution and schizophonia. (Schafer; WSP)
- **Embodied/ecological engagement**: meaning arises in the active, embodied encounter between listener and sonic environment (affordances). (Clarke)

## Glossary

- **Deep Listening** (Oliveros): a lifelong practice of "listening in every possible way to everything possible to hear" — going below the surface of sound while finding focus; combines bodywork, sonic meditations, and listening to environment/imagination/dreams.
- **Hearing vs. Listening** (Oliveros): hearing = the involuntary physical reception of sound; listening = conscious, intentional attention to what is perceived.
- **Inclusive / global / open attention** (Oliveros): expansive awareness of the whole sound field (symbol: circle).
- **Exclusive / focal attention** (Oliveros): narrowed attention to a single sound or feature (symbol: dot).
- **Sonic awareness** (Oliveros/Von Gunden): conscious, continual focusing of attention on environmental and musical sound.
- **Sonic Meditations** (Oliveros, 1971): text-score listening/sounding exercises, often eyes-closed and group-based (e.g., "Teach Yourself to Fly").
- **Sound object / objet sonore** (Schaeffer): a basic recorded unit of sonic material perceived in itself, apart from its source.
- **Reduced listening / écoute réduite** (Schaeffer): listening to a sound's intrinsic traits (pitch, timbre, grain, morphology), bracketing cause and meaning; requires repetition.
- **Acousmatic** (Schaeffer): heard without seeing the cause; sound behind a "veil" (from Pythagoras's akousmatikoi).
- **Four modes/functions of listening** (Schaeffer): *ouïr* (to hear/detect), *écouter* (to listen for the source), *entendre* (to perceive selected qualities), *comprendre* (to understand meaning).
- **Causal listening** (Chion): listening to identify a sound's source/cause.
- **Semantic listening** (Chion): listening to interpret a code/language (e.g., speech).
- **Spectromorphology** (Smalley): analysis of the spectral shape and morphology of sounds as experienced.
- **Source bonding** (Smalley): the tendency to relate sounds to their (perceived) sources.
- **Soundscape** (Schafer/Southworth): the acoustic environment as perceived by humans, in context.
- **Keynote** (Schafer): background sound that shapes a place's character, usually heard unconsciously.
- **Signal** (Schafer): a foreground sound listened to consciously (bell, siren).
- **Soundmark** (Schafer): a sound unique to a community/place, worth protecting.
- **Hi-fi / lo-fi soundscape** (Schafer): favorable vs. poor signal-to-noise environments (rural clarity vs. urban density).
- **Clairaudience** (Schafer): clear, exceptional hearing of environmental sound; an aim of ear cleaning.
- **Ear cleaning** (Schafer): graduated exercises to sensitize and re-engage the listening ear.
- **Schizophonia** (Schafer): the split of a sound from its original source (recording/amplification).
- **Soundwalk** (Westerkamp/WSP): an excursion whose main purpose is listening to the environment.
- **Geophony / biophony / anthropophony** (Krause): natural-physical, non-human-biological, and human sound sources.
- **Affordance** (Gibson/Clarke): what the (sonic) environment offers/invites the perceiver to do or feel.
- **Ecological (direct) perception** (Gibson/Clarke): meaning is picked up directly from structured stimulation, not inferred.
- **Structural listening** (Adorno): grasping the whole musical work as a coherent structure, following part-to-whole relations in real time.
- **Regression of listening** (Adorno): distracted, atomistic, hook-driven listening fostered by the culture industry.
- **Three planes of listening** (Copland): sensuous, expressive, and sheerly musical.
- **Repeat-listening passes**: re-hearing the same material multiple times, each pass focused on a different layer (instrument, rhythm, timbre, space, form).

## Clarifying Questions

- For the software tool: should it foreground a single tradition's vocabulary (e.g., Oliveros's inclusive/focal attention) as the primary UX metaphor, or blend traditions (Oliveros + Schaeffer + Schafer + Copland) into a multi-pass "focus" model? This affects terminology and session structure.
- Should ambient/environmental sessions assume live listening (soundwalk-style, requiring location/mic) or curated recorded soundscapes? The methods differ (soundwalk vs. acousmatic reduced listening).
