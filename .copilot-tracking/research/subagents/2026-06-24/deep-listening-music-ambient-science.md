## Findings

### 1. Cognitive & Perceptual Science of Attentive Listening

**Auditory Scene Analysis (Albert Bregman, 1990)**
- ASA is the process by which the human auditory system organizes raw sound into perceptually meaningful elements. Term coined by psychologist Albert Bregman (book: *Auditory Scene Analysis: The Perceptual Organization of Sound*, MIT Press, 1990). Source: Wikipedia "Auditory scene analysis."
- The eardrum vibrates as a whole; the brain must solve the problem of which frequency components belong to which source. Sounds are either "integrated" (heard as a whole, like harmony) or "segregated" into components (like counterpoint). A bell or a chord can be heard as a single "color" or as separable components.
- When sounds are grouped into a perceived sequence distinct from co-occurring sequences, each is an "auditory stream." Three key aspects: segmentation, integration, segregation. Grouping follows Gestalt-like principles (sequential grouping across time; simultaneous grouping across frequency). Grouping can be cue-based (perceptual) or "schema-based" (learned patterns) — relevant to how training/attention reshapes perception.
- Stream segregation is promoted by differences in frequency, fundamental frequency, spectral composition, and source location, and by faster sequence speed. Perception of melody and rhythm depends on notes falling in the same stream; "We tend to hear the rhythms among notes that are in the same stream, excluding those in other streams." Even perceived loudness and spatial location can be affected by sequential grouping. => Attention/grouping literally changes what is perceived.
- Skilled musicians/conductors highly develop the ability to segregate and follow multiple instrumental lines simultaneously (an attentional skill). Some ASA capabilities are present in newborns (built-in), but schema-based segregation is learned.

**Cocktail Party Effect / Selective Attention (Cherry 1953; Broadbent; Treisman)**
- The cocktail party effect: the brain focuses attention on a particular (usually auditory) stimulus, excluding other stimuli from conscious awareness — e.g., following one conversation in a noisy room. Coined "cocktail party problem" by Colin Cherry (1953), *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* 25(5):975–79. Source: Wikipedia "Cocktail party effect."
- Demonstrates that listeners can portion the totality of sound into distinct streams and decide which are most pertinent. Works best binaurally (both ears); supports sound localization and signal extraction from noise.
- Models of attention: Broadbent's filter model (early selection — a filter blocks unattended info; selection on physical characteristics like location/volume); Treisman's attenuation model (unattended info is weakened, not blocked; salient words like one's own name have low thresholds and break through); Deutsch–Norman late-selection (meaning processed before final selection); Kahneman's capacity model (attention is a limited resource distributed by arousal + allocation policy; Yerkes–Dodson: performance optimal at moderate arousal, poor when over/under-aroused; over-arousal from too-numerous/complex background sounds degrades discrimination). => Attention is selective AND capacity-limited; environment complexity and arousal level shape what we can attend to.
- Kahneman: attention governed by "momentary intentions" (conscious decision to attend) vs "enduring dispositions" (automatic capture). Focused, intentional attention costs more resources than automatic attention. => Deep listening is effortful, intentional allocation.

**Mere-Exposure Effect & Repetition (Zajonc 1968; Bornstein 1989 meta-analysis)**
- Mere-exposure effect (Robert Zajonc, 1968, *J. Personality and Social Psychology* 9(2):1–27): repeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking, even without conscious recognition. Demonstrated with sounds, words, images, etc. "Mere repeated exposure of the individual to a stimulus is a sufficient condition for the enhancement of his attitude toward it." Source: Wikipedia "Mere-exposure effect."
- Bornstein (1989) meta-analysis of 208 experiments: effect is robust and reliable (effect size r≈0.26). Strongest when stimuli are unfamiliar and presented briefly. **Maximum effect typically within 10–20 presentations; liking can DECLINE after many repetitions (inverted-U).** "People generally like a song more after they have heard it a few times, but many repetitions can reduce this preference." A delay between exposure and measurement can increase the effect.
- Mechanism: repeated exposure increases **perceptual fluency** (ease of processing), and fluency increases positive affect (Reber, Winkielman & Schwarz 1998; Bornstein & D'Agostino 1994). Zajonc: affective responses can precede/operate independently of cognition ("preferences need no inferences"). => Repetition builds familiarity and liking up to a point; over-repetition can satiate.

**Repetition & "Imagined Participation" (Elizabeth Hellmuth Margulis, *On Repeat: How Music Plays the Mind*, 2013)**
- Source: Margulis, "One more time" (Aeon essay, 7 March 2014, aeon.co) summarizing her book *On Repeat* (Oxford University Press, 2013). Margulis directs the music cognition lab (Univ. of Arkansas; now Princeton).
- Repetitiveness is a near-universal feature of music worldwide (ethnomusicologist Bruno Nettl). David Huron estimates that for **>90% of the time spent listening to music, people are hearing passages they have heard before.** => Repetition is fundamental to musical experience, not a defect.
- **Speech-to-song illusion** (Diana Deutsch): an ordinary spoken phrase, when looped several times, comes to be *heard* as singing. Demonstrates that repetition itself can shift perception from semantic meaning toward the contour (pitch patterns) and rhythm of sound — i.e., a switch into a "musical" mode of listening.
- **"Listening along with" rather than "listening to":** part of hearing something musically is to *participate imaginatively* — the mind anticipates and completes the next phrase, invites humming/tapping/swaying ("air guitar," singing along). Repetition is the key to this participatory engagement; Margulis's lab found exact-repetition rondos elicited more tendency to tap/sing along than varied ones.
- Repetition makes even random tone sequences sound more "musical" (looped sequences rated more musical even without explicit memory of them); and repetition inserted into avant-garde (Berio, Carter) excerpts made them more enjoyable, more interesting, and judged "more likely composed by a human" — "repetition serves as a handprint of human intent."
- **Semantic satiation** parallel: repeating a word (e.g. "lollipop") detaches sound from meaning and makes sensory attributes salient — repetition "makes a new way of listening possible, a more direct confrontation with the sensory attributes" of the sound. Connection to ritual (Boyer & Liénard 2008): repetition shifts attention from the overall pattern to component gestures/details.
- Familiarity itself drives emotion: Pereira et al. (Univ. Helsinki, 2011, PLOS ONE "Music and Emotions in the Brain: Familiarity Matters") — the brain shows more activity in emotional regions for familiar music **regardless of whether the listener likes it.** Alf Gabrielsson's "strong experiences with music" often involve transcendence/dissolved boundaries, partly explained by repetition's shift in attention and heightened involvement.
- Caveat: repetition's musicalizing power seems specific to sound (transferring rhythm/periodicity to flashing lights is weaker); repetition doesn't explain everything (a single bowed violin note is musical; minor chords sound dark) — it explains the participatory, "pulled along" quality of musical listening.

**Music Cognition Basics (Daniel Levitin, *This Is Your Brain on Music*, 2006)**
- Source: Wikipedia "This Is Your Brain on Music"; Levitin, *This Is Your Brain on Music: The Science of a Human Obsession* (Dutton/Penguin, 2006). Popular-science synthesis of music neuroscience for lay readers (>1M copies; praised by Oliver Sacks). Evidence: secondary/popular but grounded in the author's research and the wider literature.
- Explains music's building blocks — **timbre, rhythm, pitch, harmony** (also melody, loudness, tempo, meter) — and ties them to neuroanatomy, neurochemistry, cognitive psychology, and evolution. A central focus is **cognitive models of categorization and expectation**, and how music "exploits" these processes (anticipation/prediction as a core source of musical engagement and pleasure).
- Argues against Steven Pinker's "auditory cheesecake" (music as evolutionary by-product), positing music as an honest signal of cognitive/emotional/physical fitness and a force for social bonding (citing Darwin, Geoffrey Miller). => Listening engages categorization + prediction circuitry; supports designing for attentive tracking of timbre/pitch/rhythm/harmony and for expectation.
- Related concepts in the same tradition useful for a listening tool: **chunking** (grouping notes into perceptual units — basis of phrase perception and memory), **schemas** (learned stylistic expectations), **earworms**, and **auditory imagery** (mentally "hearing" music). Levitin (and Janata) work on music-evoked autobiographical memory underlies the episodic-memory mechanism of musical emotion.

**Expectation & Prediction — ITPRA Theory (David Huron, *Sweet Anticipation*, 2006)**
- Source: Huron, D. (2006). *Sweet Anticipation: Music and the Psychology of Expectation.* MIT Press (won the Society for Music Theory's Wallace Berry Award). See also Wikipedia "David Huron" and "Melodic expectation" ("Huron's theory of general expectation"); builds on Leonard Meyer (1956), *Emotion and Meaning in Music* (music's emotive power comes from generating, suspending, prolonging, or violating expectations). Evidence: theoretical synthesis grounded in psychology; highly influential, broadly accepted.
- **ITPRA** = five functionally distinct response systems engaged around an anticipated event — two *pre-outcome*, three *post-outcome*:
  1. **Imagination** (pre) — imagining/anticipating future outcomes shapes present behavior and motivates listening (e.g., looking forward to a favorite passage).
  2. **Tension** (pre) — physiological and attentional arousal ramps up in the moments before an expected event (preparatory readiness); peaks as the event approaches.
  3. **Prediction** (post) — the brain *rewards accurate prediction*; correctly anticipated events feel good (the "prediction effect"). Explains why familiar/repetitive/schematic music is pleasurable (links to mere-exposure and Margulis).
  4. **Reaction** (post) — a fast, automatic, conservative (often defensive/negative) response to the actual outcome, before conscious thought.
  5. **Appraisal** (post) — slower, conscious evaluation of the outcome.
- **Contrastive valence / limbic contrast:** a fast negative Reaction followed by a positive Appraisal yields a net *pleasurable* thrill — e.g., a startling but ultimately safe musical event produces relief/exhilaration. Huron uses this to explain frisson, awe, and laughter, and why "safe" violations of expectation are enjoyable. => Deep listening pleasure comes from the interplay of prediction (familiarity) and surprise (violation) within a safe context.
- Practical reading for a tool: rewarding both *prediction* (repetition, familiarity, learnable patterns) and *well-formed surprise* (expectation violations, modulations, dynamic shifts) maximizes engagement; tension/anticipation can be cultivated by directing attention to what comes next.

### 2. Psychological & Emotional Benefits and States

**Flow (Csíkszentmihályi, 1975/1990)**
- Flow = mental state of complete absorption in an activity, energized focus, full involvement and enjoyment in the process; "the melting together of action and consciousness." Introduced by Mihály Csíkszentmihályi (*Beyond Boredom and Anxiety*, 1975; *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*, 1990). Source: Wikipedia "Flow (psychology)."
- Six components (Nakamura & Csíkszentmihályi): (1) intense focused concentration on the present moment; (2) merging of action and awareness; (3) loss of reflective self-consciousness; (4) sense of personal control/agency; (5) distorted sense of time; (6) experience as intrinsically rewarding (autotelic).
- Three preconditions: clear goals/progress; clear immediate feedback; balance between perceived challenge and perceived skill. Imbalance produces anxiety (challenge > skill) or boredom (skill > challenge) or apathy (both low). One is most likely to experience flow at moderate arousal.
- **Caveat for passive listening:** "Passive activities such as taking a bath or even watching TV usually do not elicit a flow experience because active engagement is a prerequisite to entering the flow state." => Pure passive listening may not reach classic flow; deep listening must be made *active* (effortful attention, goals, tracking elements) to approach flow-like absorption.
- Related but distinct construct: **absorption** (a trait/state of immersed attention) and "effortless attention"/"cognitive absorption" (joy, control, curiosity, focused immersion, temporal dissociation). Marty-Dugas & Smilek (2019) examine "deep, effortless concentration." => For listening, "absorption" may be the more directly applicable construct than full flow.
- Evidence quality: Flow is well established but criticized for fuzzy operationalization and measurement (mostly retrospective self-report); causal claims (flow→performance) are mixed and often reciprocal. Note the Wikipedia article carries an LLM-content warning — treat specifics cautiously; rely on Csíkszentmihályi's primary works and Nakamura & Csíkszentmihályi (2001/2002, *Handbook of Positive Psychology*).

**Aesthetic Chills / Frisson ("skin orgasm")**
- Frisson (aesthetic chills, psychogenic shivers) = a psychophysiological response to rewarding stimuli (music, film, stories, beauty), felt as tingling/shivers on the spine, neck, shoulders, arms, often with goosebumps (piloerection) and pupil dilation; short-lived (seconds). Psychological pleasure mediated by the **reward system**; physiological components by the **sympathetic nervous system**. Source: Wikipedia "Frisson."
- **Trigger:** passages that violate or resolve musical expectation — unexpected harmonies, modulations, appoggiaturas, sudden dynamic/loud passages, very high/low or rapidly varying sounds. Grounded in Leonard Meyer (*Emotion and Meaning in Music*, 1956): music evokes emotion by meeting and breaking expectations. Huron (*Sweet Anticipation*, 2006) develops the psychology of expectation.
- **Neuroscience (well-supported):** Blood & Zatorre (2001, *PNAS*) — intensely pleasurable music "chills" correlate with activity in reward/emotion regions (ventral striatum/nucleus accumbens, orbitofrontal cortex, insula, anterior cingulate). Chills involve dopamine release in the striatum (caudate and nucleus accumbens). Chills reliably increase electrodermal activity (skin conductance) via sympathetic activation; intensity of tingling correlates with magnitude of sympathetic arousal and with reward-region activity.
- Sachs, Ellis, Schlaug & Loui (2016): people who experience chills have greater white-matter connectivity between auditory cortex and emotional/reward processing regions (anterior insula). Frisson is associated with the personality trait **openness to experience** (McCrae 2007; Colver & El-Alayli 2016).
- Mori & Iwanaga (2017, *Scientific Reports*): two distinct peak responses — **chills** (arousing, pleasure, increased EDA; song perceived as both happy and sad) vs **tears** (calming, slow respiration; song perceived as sad). Both induce pleasure and deep breathing. => Peak aesthetic emotion has at least two physiological signatures (arousing vs calming).
- Environment matters: frisson can be enhanced by music amplitude and cooler room/cinema temperature, and by social/narrative context. => Listening environment shapes intensity of peak experiences.
- Caveat: self-reported chills do not reliably correspond to observable piloerection (McPhetres et al. 2024) — they may be partly independent; subjective report is the more robust measure.

**Mechanisms of Musical Emotion — BRECVEMA (Juslin & Västfjäll 2008; Juslin 2013)**
- Source: Wikipedia "Music and emotion"; primary: Juslin & Västfjäll (2008) "Emotional responses to music: The need to consider underlying mechanisms," *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 31(5):559–575; Juslin (2013) *Physics of Life Reviews* (adds Aesthetic judgment → BRECVEMA). Eight mechanisms by which music induces (not just expresses) emotion:
  1. **Brain stem reflex** — sudden, loud, dissonant, or fast-rising sounds are taken by the brain stem to signal an urgent event → arousal. (Relevant to ambient/environmental sound.)
  2. **Rhythmic entrainment** — music's rhythm entrains internal bodily rhythms (e.g., heart rate), shifting emotional state.
  3. **Evaluative conditioning** — music repeatedly paired with positive/negative events comes to evoke that emotion.
  4. **Emotional contagion** — the listener perceives the emotion expressed by the music and "catches"/feels it internally.
  5. **Visual imagery** — music evokes inner images that drive emotion.
  6. **Episodic memory** — music triggers an autobiographical memory ("Darling, they are playing our tune").
  7. **Musical expectancy** — a musical feature violates, delays, or confirms learned expectations (the Meyer/Huron mechanism; basis of tension–resolution and chills).
  8. **Aesthetic judgment** (added 2013) — evaluation of the music's aesthetic value (beauty, skill, novelty, message).
- => Different mechanisms can be deliberately engaged: contagion and entrainment work fast/bodily; episodic memory and imagery are personal; expectancy rewards attentive structural listening. A listening guide can cue any of these.
- Perceived vs felt emotion: emotions are "less often conveyed by music than elicited, but more intense than elicited emotions when conveyed"; perceived emotions are more consistent across people than felt ones. Listener's current mood biases felt emotion; more neurotic listeners have stronger sad reactions, more agreeable listeners stronger reactions overall.

**Structural Features → Perceived Emotion (well-replicated associations)**
- Source: Wikipedia "Music and emotion" (summarizing Gomez & Danuser 2007; Hevner 1935; Lahdelma & Eerola 2016; etc.).
  - **Tempo**: faster → higher arousal and slightly higher valence; fast pieces judged longer than slow same-duration pieces.
  - **Mode**: major → happiness/joy/higher valence; minor → sadness, more dissonant, more melancholy/tension. (Major/minor associations are largely *learned*/cultural; voice-like cues — low+slow=sad, fast+high=happy — appear more innate.)
  - **Loudness**: louder → higher arousal. **Pitch**: more pitch variation → higher arousal. **Bass**: high-intensity bass increases physiological arousal and subjective emotional intensity (subcortical pathways → "visceral" feel; Epain et al. 2026).
  - **Harmony**: resolving harmonies → tension-and-release; unexpected harmonies → surprise; large surprise/uncertainty in a final chord → stronger bodily sensation (Daikoku et al. 2024, bodily maps).
  - **Articulation**: legato → cohesive, calm, sad, scary; staccato → tense, energetic, amusing, surprising.
  - **Lyrics**: music without lyrics tends to elicit only basic emotions (happiness, sadness), rarely social emotions (anger, shame, jealousy).
  - **Mixed cues**: happy+sad cues (e.g., major key + slow tempo) can produce simultaneous happy AND sad feelings (bittersweet). => attentive listeners can hold complex/mixed affect.
- Arousing music → ↑ heart rate and muscle tension; calming music → ↓ heart rate, ↓ muscle tension, ↑ skin temperature. Listening to music increases dopamine and decreases cortisol. => Listening can be designed for either activation or calming.

**Familiarity and Live vs Recorded**
- Familiar music induces stronger emotional/brain responses (Pereira et al. 2011; Ali & Peynircioglu 2010). Because music is processed at lower sensory levels, familiar excerpts can trigger emotional memories resistant to memory distortion.
- Live performances evoke stronger bodily/emotional responses than recordings of the same songs (Swarbrick et al. 2019, *Frontiers in Psychology*, head-movement study) — personal connection and crowd participation intensify the experience. => For a recorded-audio tool, compensate via active engagement, good playback quality (incl. bass/spatial), and personal/contextual framing.

**Attention Restoration Theory (Rachel & Stephen Kaplan, 1989/1995) — relevant to ambient/nature listening**
- Source: Wikipedia "Attention restoration theory"; primary: Kaplan & Kaplan (1989) *The Experience of Nature*; Kaplan (1995) *Journal of Environmental Psychology* 15:169–182.
- Directed (voluntary, effortful, goal-oriented) attention fatigues with sustained use ("directed attention fatigue"). Natural settings evoke **"soft fascination"** — effortless, involuntary attention to gentle, interesting stimuli (clouds drifting, leaves rustling, **water flowing**, birdsong) — which lets directed-attention resources replenish while leaving room for reflection. Contrast: "hard fascination" (sports, thrilling films, video games) fully captures attention, leaving little reflective capacity.
- Four properties of a restorative setting: **being away** (distance from routine demands), **extent** (enough scope/coherence to invite exploration — relevant to immersive soundscapes), **compatibility** (fit between setting and the person's purpose), **fascination** (interest without effort).
- Companion theory: **Stress Reduction Theory** (Ulrich 1991) — nature drives emotional/physiological stress recovery independent of cognitive benefits; Kaplan later treated ART + SRT as complementary (shared mind–body/vagal pathway).
- Evidence quality (mixed→moderate): executive attention and working memory show the most consistent restoration; alerting/basic attention less so. Benefits more consistent for exposures ≥30 min (diminishing returns after); ≥120 min/week in nature associated with better health/wellbeing (White et al. 2019, *Scientific Reports*; plateau ~200–300 min). Nature exposure reduces rumination and subgenual prefrontal cortex activity (Bratman et al. 2015, *PNAS*). Default Mode Network proposed as a mechanism. **Virtual/mediated nature** (images, VR — and by extension recordings) can still increase restorativeness, positive affect, and attention, moderated by immersion quality, duration, and realism. => Supports value of attentive *recorded* nature-sound listening, with realism/immersion as moderators.

**Mindfulness & Contemplative / Sound Meditation (stress reduction, relaxation)**
- Sound/receptive meditation as a low-barrier path to the **"relaxation response"** (Herbert Benson) — parasympathetic activation, lowered blood pressure, countering fight-or-flight. Source: Goldsby, Goldsby, McWalters & Mills (2017), "Effects of Singing Bowl Sound Meditation on Mood, Tension, and Well-being: An Observational Study," *Journal of Evidence-Based Complementary & Alternative Medicine* 22(3):401–406 (PMC5871151).
- Findings (n=62, ~60-min Tibetan singing-bowl sound meditation; pre/post): significant reductions in **tension, anger, fatigue, depressed mood, and anxiety** (all p<.001; large effect sizes), and a significant **increase in spiritual well-being**. People previously *naïve* to the practice showed *larger* tension reductions than experienced participants. Participants could simply lie down and even fall asleep — i.e., effective with minimal training/discipline (vs. focused mindfulness).
- Evidence quality: **weak–moderate** — observational, no control group, mostly female convenience sample; self-report mood scales (POMS, HADS, FACIT-SP). Proposed mechanisms (binaural beats, "biofield") are speculative; the robust part is the pre/post mood/tension improvement and the relaxation-response framing. A small RCT (Landry 2014) found a single Himalayan bowl lowered blood pressure/heart rate more than silence.
- Broader context: the wider mindfulness-meditation literature (e.g., MBSR; Cahn & Polich 2006 review of meditation EEG/neuroimaging) supports relaxation and anxiety reduction; "observe sensations without judging them" instructions in the sound-meditation protocol mirror mindfulness. => Non-judgmental, receptive attention to sound is a plausible, low-effort route to relaxation and present-moment focus; especially effective for newcomers. Related artistic framework: Pauline Oliveros's "Deep Listening" (contemplative listening practice).

### 3. Benefits Specific to Ambient / Environmental / Soundscape Listening

**Acoustic Ecology / Soundscape Studies (R. Murray Schafer; World Soundscape Project)**
- Source: Wikipedia "Acoustic ecology." Discipline founded late 1960s by composer R. Murray Schafer with the World Soundscape Project at Simon Fraser University (team incl. Barry Truax, Hildegard Westerkamp). Studies the relationship between humans and environment *mediated through sound*.
- Schafer's vocabulary (from *The Tuning of the World* / *The Soundscape*): **keynote sounds** (prevalent, often-overlooked background sounds, e.g. traffic), **sound signals** (foreground sounds listened to consciously), **soundmarks** (a community's unique, distinctive sounds — analogous to landmarks). A soundscape comprises **geophony** (earth/wind/water), **biophony** (animal/bird sounds), **anthrophony** (human-made sound). Schafer also coined "schizophonia" (the split of a sound from its source via recording/reproduction) — directly relevant to listening to *recorded* environmental sound.
- Bernie Krause's **acoustic niche hypothesis** (1993): species partition the acoustic spectrum by frequency/time to communicate without competition; healthy mature ecosystems show well-partitioned, "orchestrated" soundscapes (biophony like an orchestra). => Attentive listening can reveal the layered structure of a natural soundscape.
- Visual–auditory interaction: pleasant visual surroundings increase tolerance/appreciation of an environment's sounds; sound preference depends on familiarity and cultural/practical context. => Context and framing shape whether ambient sound is heard as pleasant.
- Note: acoustic ecology is descriptive/ecological/compositional rather than a wellbeing-outcomes literature; wellbeing claims come more from environmental psychology (ART/SRT) and nature-sound health studies (below). Related practitioner tradition: **Pauline Oliveros's "Deep Listening"** — distinguishes *hearing* (passive reception) from *listening* (active, inclusive attention to sound) — an influential contemplative-listening framework, more artistic than empirical.

**Natural-Sound Health Benefits (empirical — strongest direct evidence for ambient listening)**
- **Buxton, Pearson, Allou, Fristrup & Wittemyer (2021), *PNAS* 118(14):e2013097118** — "A synthesis of the health benefits of natural sounds and their distribution in national parks." Systematic review + meta-analysis (36 publications; 18 meta-analyzed). Natural sounds produced **decreased stress and annoyance** (Hedges' g = −0.60, 95% CI −0.97 to −0.23) and **improved health and positive affect** (g = 1.63, 95% CI 0.09 to 3.16). Specific beneficial outcomes: **decreased pain, lower stress, improved mood, enhanced cognitive performance.** Notably, **water sounds** were most associated with positive affective/health outcomes, while **bird sounds** were most associated with stress/annoyance reduction. => Strong, citable support that attentive listening to natural soundscapes (incl. recordings) benefits stress, mood, pain, and cognition.
- **Gould van Praag, Garfinkel, ... Critchley (2017), *Scientific Reports* 7:45273** — "Mind-wandering and alterations to default mode network connectivity when listening to naturalistic versus artificial sounds." Listening to **natural** sounds (vs artificial) shifted DMN functional connectivity (anterior→posterior midline coupling) and increased **high-frequency heart-rate variability (parasympathetic / 'rest-and-digest' activity)**, consistent with Stress Recovery Theory; subjective effects and attentional focus shifted **outward** with natural sounds vs **inward** (self-referential, rumination-prone) with artificial sounds. Individual baseline differences moderated the response. => Mechanistic support: natural-sound listening promotes physiological relaxation and outward-directed attention.
- Caveat: natural-sound wellbeing effects are well-supported in aggregate, but effect sizes vary widely (note the wide CI on positive affect), studies are heterogeneous, and individual differences matter. "Naturalness"/preference and perceived restorativeness moderate outcomes.

### 4. Evidence Quality Summary (well-supported vs speculative)

- **Well-supported (peer-reviewed, replicated):** Auditory scene analysis & stream segregation (Bregman); selective/capacity-limited attention (Cherry, Broadbent, Treisman, Kahneman); mere-exposure effect (Bornstein 1989 meta-analysis, r≈0.26, inverted-U); structural-feature→emotion associations (tempo/mode/loudness; Hevner, Gomez & Danuser); music-chills neuroscience (Blood & Zatorre 2001 PNAS; dopamine/striatal reward); familiarity→emotion (Pereira 2011); BRECVEMA as an organizing framework (Juslin & Västfjäll 2008, highly cited); natural-sound health benefits in aggregate (Buxton 2021 PNAS meta-analysis; Gould van Praag 2017 HRV/DMN); nature restoration on attention/mood/rumination (Bratman 2015; White 2019).
- **Strong theory, partly empirical / influential synthesis:** Huron's ITPRA & expectation (2006); Meyer (1956); Margulis's repetition/imagined-participation (lab studies + book); flow (well-established construct but fuzzy operationalization, retrospective self-report, mixed causal evidence); ART soft-fascination (consistent for executive attention/working memory, less so for basic attention).
- **Weaker / preliminary / popular:** Singing-bowl sound meditation (Goldsby 2017: observational, no control, self-report) — directionally promising for tension/anxiety but low-rigor; mechanistic claims about binaural beats/"biofield" are speculative. Levitin's book is reputable popular science, not primary data. Self-reported chills vs piloerection dissociate (McPhetres 2024). Note the Wikipedia "Flow" article carries an LLM-content warning — verify specifics against primary sources.

## Implications for a Deep Listening Tool

*Research insights (evidence-grounded), not product specs:*

- **Make listening active, not passive.** Passive reception rarely yields flow/absorption; effortful, intentional attention does. Encouraging users to *direct* attention (track an instrument, follow a melodic line, notice timbre) engages auditory scene analysis and selective attention, which literally changes what is perceived (Bregman; Cherry/Kahneman; Csíkszentmihályi).
- **Design for absorption more than "classic flow."** A challenge–skill balance, clear focus targets, and freedom from distraction support absorption; over-complex scenes can over-arouse and degrade discrimination (Yerkes–Dodson/Kahneman). Aim for moderate arousal and a single clear focus at a time.
- **Leverage repetition and re-listening.** Familiarity reliably increases liking (peaking ~10–20 exposures, then satiating) and strengthens emotional/brain responses regardless of liking. Repeated/looped listening fosters "imagined participation" (anticipating, completing, moving along) and a shift from meaning to sensory detail (Margulis; Zajonc/Bornstein; Pereira).
- **Cue prediction *and* surprise.** Pleasure comes from rewarded prediction (familiar patterns) plus safe violations of expectation (modulations, dynamic/timbral shifts) — the "contrastive valence" that drives chills (Huron ITPRA; Meyer; Blood & Zatorre). Guiding attention toward "what happens next" cultivates productive anticipation/tension.
- **Name the mechanisms of emotion you want to evoke.** BRECVEMA gives concrete levers: entrainment (move/tap to rhythm), contagion (feel the expressed emotion), imagery (evoke inner scenes), episodic memory (personal associations), expectancy (structural attention). Different prompts engage different routes (Juslin & Västfjäll).
- **Teach structural listening vocabulary.** Drawing attention to timbre, pitch, rhythm, harmony, mode, tempo, dynamics, and articulation gives users handholds and supports chunking/schema formation, deepening engagement over sessions (Levitin; music-and-emotion structural features).
- **Support two distinct target states.** (a) *Activation/peak* (chills, frisson) via expectation play, dynamics, bass, good playback, slightly cool environment; (b) *Calm/restoration* via slower tempo, legato, low arousal, and receptive non-judgmental attention (chills vs tears; relaxation response).
- **Offer a low-effort, receptive "sound meditation" mode.** Non-judgmental observation of sound ("notice sensations without judging") lowers tension/anxiety with minimal training and benefits newcomers most — a gentler on-ramp than focused mindfulness (Goldsby; Benson).
- **Use natural/ambient soundscapes for restoration and grounding.** Attentive listening to natural sounds reduces stress/annoyance and improves mood, pain, and cognition; it boosts parasympathetic activity and shifts attention *outward* (away from rumination). Favor water and birdsong; preserve realism/immersion since mediated nature works but realism moderates benefit (Buxton; Gould van Praag; ART/SRT).
- **Frame attentive environmental listening as presence/grounding.** Soundscape concepts (keynote/signal/soundmark; geophony/biophony/anthrophony; acoustic niche) give users a structure for noticing layers and foreground/background — turning ambient sound into an object of rich attention rather than backdrop (Schafer; Krause).
- **Mind dose and satiation.** Restoration benefits are more consistent for ≥30-minute exposures; repetition builds liking but can satiate. Vary material and pace sessions to avoid over-repetition fatigue while still exploiting familiarity.
- **Account for individual differences.** Trait openness predicts frisson; baseline autonomic/neural state moderates nature-sound effects; mood biases felt emotion; prior experience changes meditation benefit. Personalization (material, prompts, difficulty) is evidence-supported.
- **Compensate for "recorded, not live."** Live music moves people more; for recordings, boost engagement via active prompts, high-quality playback (including bass/spatial cues), personal/contextual framing, and good listening environment.
